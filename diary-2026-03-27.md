# Research Diary — 2026-03-27

## CortexLM E-prop: Summary of Findings and Current Directions

### Background

We are training a biologically-structured cortical language model using **e-prop** (Bellec et al. 2020), a local online learning rule that avoids backpropagation through time. The architecture features layered E/I columns, AdEx adaptive neurons, short-term synaptic plasticity, and a Modern Hopfield hippocampal module. The learning rule is intended to be biologically plausible: weights are updated using only locally available signals, with no global error gradient.

---

### The Key Finding: Apical Dendrites Are Necessary for E-prop to Work

Without the apical pathway, e-prop completely fails on this architecture. The learning signal (`∂L/∂z` — the gradient flowing back from the readout into the recurrent layers) decays from ~0.002 to near zero within the first 1–2M tokens, as the readout Adam optimizer rapidly converges to the best solution achievable with static recurrent weights (~400 ppl). Once the readout saturates, there is no gradient left to teach the recurrent weights, and training stalls permanently.

**With the additive apical pathway** (a learned L5→L23 feedback connection), the learning signal instead *grows* during training — climbing from ~0.0015 to ~0.0075 over the first 150K tokens and remaining healthy throughout. The mechanism is a positive feedback loop: as the apical weights are learned alongside the recurrent weights, a stronger readout produces stronger apical feedback, which produces a stronger learning signal, which produces better recurrent updates.

This provides a compelling computational account of why apical dendrites in cortical L5 carry top-down signals: they may be the biological implementation of feedback-based credit assignment — not merely modulatory, but structurally necessary for online learning to proceed at all.

Other interventions we tested (learning signal normalization, longer eligibility traces τ_e=50, smaller batch size) had no meaningful effect without apical, and normalization was actively harmful even with apical (it discards the magnitude of the learning signal, which encodes real calibration information about readout confidence).

---

### Sample Efficiency vs. Transformer

The performance-vs-tokens comparison is striking:

| Model | Tokens to reach ~200 ppl | Tokens to reach ~80 ppl | Final ppl |
|-------|--------------------------|--------------------------|-----------|
| Transformer (baseline) | ~20M | never within 50M tokens | ~36 @50M |
| E-prop + apical (1f) | **~150K** | **~5–10M** | ~80–85 (plateau) |

E-prop+apical reaches 200 ppl in roughly **150K tokens** — the transformer takes ~20M tokens to get there, making our model approximately **130× more sample-efficient** in the early learning phase. Our model reaches ~80 ppl within a few million tokens; the transformer doesn't achieve that until well into its run.

However, the transformer ultimately descends much further, finishing near 36 ppl at 50M tokens while e-prop+apical appears to plateau around 80–85 ppl. The architecture's full representational power (which reaches ~27–29 ppl under full BPTT) is not yet being unlocked by the online learning rule alone.

---

### The Plateau Problem and Current Strategies

The ~80 ppl ceiling is our current focus. Three candidate explanations:

1. **Batch cancellation noise.** E-prop computes the learning signal by averaging gradients across a batch of 32 sequences. Opposite-sign gradients cancel, leaving a noisy, attenuated update signal. The network bounces around a basin rather than descending cleanly into it.

2. **Limited credit horizon.** E-prop's eligibility traces look back only as far as the trace timescale (~8ms biological time). BPTT on the same architecture reaches 27–29 ppl, suggesting ~50 ppl of performance is left on the table by the online approximation alone.

3. **LR too high for the plateau.** The flat learning rate keeps taking steps that are too large for the shallow loss landscape at ~80 ppl, causing the characteristic oscillation we observe in the training curve.

**Our current approach: hybrid e-prop / BPTT consolidation.** Biologically motivated by the complementary learning systems hypothesis (McClelland et al. 1995) and hippocampal sharp-wave ripple replay: e-prop handles fast online learning during the "awake" phase, while periodic BPTT bursts act as "sleep consolidation" to correct accumulated noise and escape plateaus.

We are currently sweeping six configurations:

| Variant | BPTT trigger | Consolidation LR | Character |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| conservative | every 500 e-prop steps | 1e-5 | Rare, gentle |
| moderate | every 100 steps | 1e-4 | Balanced |
| aggressive | every 20 steps | 3e-4 | Frequent, forceful |
| adaptive (slow) | plateau detected over ~2M tokens | 3e-4 | Event-driven |
| adaptive (fast) | plateau detected over ~1M tokens | 3e-4 | Event-driven |
| adaptive (vfast) | plateau detected over ~250K tokens | 3e-4 | Hair-trigger |

The adaptive variants detect plateaus by tracking an exponential moving average of training loss: when improvement over a sliding window falls below 0.5%, a burst of 20 full-BPTT steps fires to knock the weights out of the basin. A cooldown period prevents continuous triggering.
